Whiting Sweeting


Stephentown, NY — January 3, 1791
Age: 26
Gender: Male
Ethnicity: white
Weapons:
  • a club
  • a large knife

Whiting Sweeting, the man responsible for Constable Darius Quimby's death, was a young adult resident of Stephentown at the time of the incident. Much of what is known about him comes from his own pre-execution narrative, a pamphlet he authored (or dictated) titled The Narrative of Whiting Sweeting, published in 1791 for moral and spiritual edification. In it, he frames his life as a cautionary tale of sin leading to ruin, while maintaining his innocence of premeditated murder.

The incident stemmed from a dispute over an iron kettle, which Sweeting claimed was not his doing (the original kettle was later found elsewhere). Warned by a neighbor, Stephen Bailey, that men were coming for him (possibly with a warrant), Sweeting panicked and fled to his father's house with his family. He returned home with his brother, found no one there, but while heading elsewhere, his brother was seized by the posse, prompting Sweeting to run into the woods. Surrounded by the group, whom he described as "yelling like drunk savages," Sweeting climbed a rock with a club and knife, intending only to scare them off and escape, not to kill. When Quimby and others attacked, they tumbled into the snow; Sweeting insists Quimby was wounded accidentally during the struggle, possibly by falling on the knife he held in fear, and denies any malice or intent to murder. He claims he had no prior knowledge of the warrant and forgives witnesses for any "false coloring" in their testimony.

Background

Details of Sweeting's birth and early years are vague in the narrative, but he portrays himself as having lived a life marred by moral failings from youth. He mentions a father and brother involved in the events leading to the crime, suggesting a local family in the Stephentown area, likely farmers or settlers similar to the Quimbys. Sweeting reflects on a "life spent in sin," including inattention to oaths, perjuries, and disregard for truth and justice, which he believes eroded his soul and led to his downfall. He had a wife (mentioned bidding him farewell before execution) and possibly children, as he fled with his "family" on the night of the incident, though specifics are not elaborated. His narrative serves as a confessional, warning others—especially youth—against similar vices, emphasizing how small sins escalate to catastrophe.

Aftermath

Sweeting was tried in the New York Supreme Court in July 1791 for Quimby's murder. The narrative includes an account of the proceedings, the chief justice's charge to the jury (summarized as focusing on evidence and law), and his death sentence. He disputed witness accounts but was convicted. In prison, ministers urged him to confess malice, but he refused, insisting on his truth. He attempted escape the day before execution but was recaptured, later regretting it as defying God's will. Sweeting expressed deep sorrow, humbled himself, sought spiritual counsel, and debated theology with clergy (e.g., affirming Christ's death for all, free will, and perseverance through faith). He included poems on false swearing and salvation in his narrative. Hanged in Albany on August 26, 1791, Sweeting requested his body for burial but it was dissected as per custom. At the gallows, after farewells to his wife and friends, he commended his soul to God, proclaimed justification through Christ's blood, and exhorted witnesses to truthfulness while warning against perjury. In a post-script, he reaffirmed his account's truth, appealing to heaven, and positioned his story as a moral lesson on sin's consequences.

Historical records do not mention Quimby having a wife or children of his own at the time of his death, suggesting he may have been unmarried or without documented dependents. He left behind his father, Ephraim, and likely other family members in the Stephentown area, as indicated by the 1790 census. The family's colonial roots imply siblings or relatives in New England, but specifics are sparse, emphasizing the limited documentation of ordinary lives in the era.

Jonathan Arnold, Thomas Southward, John West, & Abel Hackett


Columbia County, NY — October 22, 1791
Age: young men (no ages specified)
Gender: Male
Ethnicity: white
Weapons:
  • a pistol
  • a musket

On October 22, 1791, in Nobletown (now Hillsdale), Columbia County, Sheriff Cornelius Hogeboom was ambushed and shot through the heart while returning on horseback from serving an eviction writ related to a land dispute. Jonathan Arnold, facing auction of his property, fired a pistol signal, summoning about 17 men disguised as Native Americans (painted faces, Indian dress) who emerged from a barn, fired volleys, and pursued Hogeboom and his companions. Hogeboom refused to flee, declaring he was "vested with the law," and was fatally shot by one of the group (likely Arnold or an associate). This was amid broader tenant resistance to the manor leasehold system.

Background

The perpetrators were local tenants and squatters in the Nobletown/Hillsdale area, primarily from New England Yankee stock, who had settled on disputed lands under the patroon system (feudal-like leases held by large Dutch-American landowners like Philip Schuyler). They were involved in anti-rent unrest, resisting evictions and auctions that threatened their farms. Jonathan Arnold was specifically the debtor facing execution of his property, making him the focal point of the dispute—he was a resident farmer or settler in the region with ties to the anti-Federalist/tenant faction opposing Federalist manor owners. The group as a whole represented working-class rural settlers, often armed and organized in mobs to deter officials, reflecting post-Revolutionary tensions over land rights in the Hudson Valley. No individual accomplishments, controversies, families, or early lives are detailed beyond their roles in this event.

Aftermath

A posse was quickly formed, leading to the arrest of 13 men (including Arnold), who were indicted in December 1791—Southward as principal and nine as accessories. Four suspects fled to Nova Scotia via New London, with a £250 reward offered for their capture (outcome unclear). The trial at the Court of Oyer and Terminer in Claverack in February 1792 was described as "long and impartial," but all defendants were acquitted, possibly due to local jury sympathies with anti-Federalist tenants amid political tensions. The event shocked the community, prompting vigorous enforcement that suppressed anti-rent disturbances for over 50 years, averting a larger revolt until the mid-19th century Anti-Rent Wars. No specific laws were changed directly, but it highlighted vulnerabilities in rural law enforcement.

Beverly Anthony Allen


Augusta, GA — January 11, 1794
Age: 24
Gender: Male
Ethnicity: -
Weapons: a pistol

On January 11, 1794, in Augusta, Georgia, Marshal Forsyth (accompanied by two deputies) went to Mrs. Dixon’s boarding house—the town’s best inn—to serve civil court papers on Beverly and William Allen. A local merchant had sued the brothers for failing to pay for goods. Allen, who was in town on business (some accounts note he had also come to preach), spotted the officers, retreated upstairs, and locked himself in a room. Forsyth knocked on the door “from a principle of decency.” Allen threatened to shoot if they did not leave, then fired a pistol (one contemporary account calls it a “pistol musket”) through the door or a small opening. The ball struck Forsyth in the forehead, killing the 40-year-old marshal instantly

Background

Allen was a former Methodist Episcopal Church minister and charismatic speaker who had success in the Carolinas in the 1780s (including appointments to circuits in Georgia, Charleston, and Wilmington). He married Anna Singletary (daughter of a prominent South Carolina family). By the early 1790s he had left the pulpit to pursue business ventures, settling in the Elberton area of Georgia with his brother William. Contemporary newspapers described him harshly as “a Methodist preacher whose character is as vile as it is possible,” likely due to reputational issues tied to the financial dispute that led to the fatal encounter.

Aftermath

The deputies arrested Allen on the spot. He was tried, convicted of murder, and jailed. After about six weeks he escaped (suspected with help from friends). He briefly took refuge at his brother’s home, but a posse of about 30 men surrounded the house, threatened to burn it, and ultimately set it ablaze; Allen emerged and escaped a second time. A reward notice appeared in the Kentucky Gazette (May 31, 1794), but he was never recaptured.

Allen fled to the remote “Rogue’s Harbor” area of Logan County, Kentucky (later Russellville), a known haven for fugitives. He lived under an assumed or low-profile identity, practiced medicine (becoming known locally as Dr. Beverly Allen), and was eventually joined by his wife. He acquired significant land along Big Whippoorwill Creek, grew wealthy, retired from medicine to farm, and resumed religious teaching—heading a local school whose students included the future evangelist Peter Cartwright. An anecdote from his flight describes him, pale and disheveled after days on the run, interrupting a Presbyterian service to confess his “apostasy” and warn the congregation against falling from grace; the rough frontier crowd reportedly wept.

He died of natural causes in February 1817 (after writing his will on October 26, 1816) and was buried in the Allen Cemetery south of Russellville. No laws were changed as a direct result of the killing; it was simply recorded as the first line-of-duty death of a federal law enforcement officer. The U.S. Marshals Service did not officially close the case file until 2012, after modern historical research confirmed Allen’s identity and fate in Kentucky.
