Whiting Sweeting


Stephentown, NY — January 3, 1791

Age: 26
Gender: Male
Ethnicity: white
Weapons:
  • a club
  • a large knife

Whiting Sweeting, the man responsible for Constable Darius Quimby's death, was a young adult resident of Stephentown at the time of the incident. Much of what is known about him comes from his own pre-execution narrative, a pamphlet he authored (or dictated) titled The Narrative of Whiting Sweeting, published in 1791 for moral and spiritual edification. In it, he frames his life as a cautionary tale of sin leading to ruin, while maintaining his innocence of premeditated murder.

The incident stemmed from a dispute over an iron kettle, which Sweeting claimed was not his doing (the original kettle was later found elsewhere). Warned by a neighbor, Stephen Bailey, that men were coming for him (possibly with a warrant), Sweeting panicked and fled to his father's house with his family. He returned home with his brother, found no one there, but while heading elsewhere, his brother was seized by the posse, prompting Sweeting to run into the woods. Surrounded by the group, whom he described as "yelling like drunk savages," Sweeting climbed a rock with a club and knife, intending only to scare them off and escape, not to kill. When Quimby and others attacked, they tumbled into the snow; Sweeting insists Quimby was wounded accidentally during the struggle, possibly by falling on the knife he held in fear, and denies any malice or intent to murder. He claims he had no prior knowledge of the warrant and forgives witnesses for any "false coloring" in their testimony.

Background

Details of Sweeting's birth and early years are vague in the narrative, but he portrays himself as having lived a life marred by moral failings from youth. He mentions a father and brother involved in the events leading to the crime, suggesting a local family in the Stephentown area, likely farmers or settlers similar to the Quimbys. Sweeting reflects on a "life spent in sin," including inattention to oaths, perjuries, and disregard for truth and justice, which he believes eroded his soul and led to his downfall. He had a wife (mentioned bidding him farewell before execution) and possibly children, as he fled with his "family" on the night of the incident, though specifics are not elaborated. His narrative serves as a confessional, warning others—especially youth—against similar vices, emphasizing how small sins escalate to catastrophe.

Aftermath

Sweeting was tried in the New York Supreme Court in July 1791 for Quimby's murder. The narrative includes an account of the proceedings, the chief justice's charge to the jury (summarized as focusing on evidence and law), and his death sentence. He disputed witness accounts but was convicted. In prison, ministers urged him to confess malice, but he refused, insisting on his truth. He attempted escape the day before execution but was recaptured, later regretting it as defying God's will. Sweeting expressed deep sorrow, humbled himself, sought spiritual counsel, and debated theology with clergy (e.g., affirming Christ's death for all, free will, and perseverance through faith). He included poems on false swearing and salvation in his narrative. Hanged in Albany on August 26, 1791, Sweeting requested his body for burial but it was dissected as per custom. At the gallows, after farewells to his wife and friends, he commended his soul to God, proclaimed justification through Christ's blood, and exhorted witnesses to truthfulness while warning against perjury. In a post-script, he reaffirmed his account's truth, appealing to heaven, and positioned his story as a moral lesson on sin's consequences.

Historical records do not mention Quimby having a wife or children of his own at the time of his death, suggesting he may have been unmarried or without documented dependents. He left behind his father, Ephraim, and likely other family members in the Stephentown area, as indicated by the 1790 census. The family's colonial roots imply siblings or relatives in New England, but specifics are sparse, emphasizing the limited documentation of ordinary lives in the era.
