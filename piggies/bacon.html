Constable Darius Quimby


Stephentown, NY — January 3, 1791
Age: 20
Gender: Male
Cause of Death: stabbed
Killed By: Whiting Sweeting

Darius Quimby holds the distinction of being recognized as the first known law enforcement officer killed in the line of duty in the United States, an event that occurred shortly after the nation's founding and has been memorialized in various historical accounts and police honors. His story is often cited in discussions of early American policing and the dangers faced by constables in frontier communities.

Background

Quimby was born around 1760 (though some accounts suggest circa 1771) in or near Stephentown, which was then part of Albany County, New York (now Rensselaer County). He was the son of Ephraim Quimby (circa 1730–1824), a settler in the region who married Elisabeth Pepper around 1760. The Quimby family traced its roots to English immigrants William and Robert Quinby, who arrived in New England in the 1630s, and they were part of the broader colonial settlement in New York and New England during the post-Revolutionary era. The 1790 U.S. Census records Ephraim as head of household in Stephentown, Albany County, listing Darius as a free white male over 16, confirming his adulthood and eligibility for community roles like constable. Little is documented about his siblings or extended family beyond this, but the Quimbys were typical of frontier families involved in farming and local trades.

Specific motivations for Quimby becoming a constable are not detailed in historical records, but constables in early America were often community members elected or appointed to maintain order, serve warrants, and handle minor disputes—roles that were typically unpaid or minimally compensated. As a young man in his 20s or early 30s living in a rural, post-Revolutionary settlement, he likely took on the position as a civic duty, common for eligible males in small towns where formal policing was rudimentary. Prior to his death, Quimby was engaged in manual labor, such as making wood shingles with two other men, indicating he was not a full-time officer but rather a part-time or volunteer peacekeeper.

His primary "accomplishment" is posthumous: being etched into history as the inaugural line-of-duty death for U.S. law enforcement, a milestone that has led to his name appearing on memorials, such as those honoring fallen officers. There are no records of notable prior achievements, arrests, or cases he handled, reflecting the modest scope of constabulary work in 1790s rural New York. His brief service underscores the informal nature of early American law enforcement, where ordinary citizens like Quimby stepped up for community needs.

Death

On January 3, 1791, Quimby was part of a posse led by Constable John McCarty attempting to serve a trespassing warrant on Whiting Sweeting, a 26-year-old local resident, in the area near Stephentown. Quimby encountered the group while working on shingles and voluntarily joined the pursuit. Sweeting, upon spotting the posse, fled into the snowy woods at night and climbed onto a rock, arming himself with a club and knife while threatening to harm anyone who approached. Undeterred, Quimby grabbed Sweeting and pulled him down, leading to a violent struggle in the snow. During the scuffle, Sweeting stabbed Quimby twice in the right side with a knife, inflicting fatal wounds. Quimby succumbed to his injuries shortly after, marking the incident as a stabbing death in the line of duty.

Aftermath

Historical records do not mention Quimby having a wife or children of his own at the time of his death, suggesting he may have been unmarried or without documented dependents. He left behind his father, Ephraim, and likely other family members in the Stephentown area, as indicated by the 1790 census. The family's colonial roots imply siblings or relatives in New England, but specifics are sparse, emphasizing the limited documentation of ordinary lives in the era.

Quimby's death prompted swift legal action: Sweeting was arrested, tried for murder in the New York Supreme Court during the July 1791 term, convicted, and hanged publicly in Albany on August 26, 1791. The case highlighted the perils of early law enforcement and set a precedent for recognizing officer deaths. In modern times, Quimby's legacy is preserved through memorials, such as the National Law Enforcement Officers Memorial, and local commemorations like exhibits at the Stephentown Heritage Center. His story has been referenced in police training, historical writings, and annual remembrances during National Police Week, serving as a symbol of sacrifice in the nascent United States.

Sheriff Cornelius Hogeboom


Columbia County, NY — October 22, 1791
Age: 52
Gender: Male
Cause of Death: shot
Killed By: Jonathan Arnold and 16 others

Sheriff Cornelius Hogeboom was ambushed and fatally shot through the heart while performing his duties in Nobletown (now part of Hillsdale), Columbia County, New York. He was returning from serving legal processes or adjourning an auction related to a land dispute when a signal pistol shot by Jonathan Arnold summoned a group of about 17 men disguised as Native Americans (a tactic for anonymity during anti-rent protests). The attackers emerged from a barn, pursued Hogeboom and his companions on horseback, and one fired the lethal shot. Hogeboom, refusing to flee and declaring, "I am vested with the law, and they should never find me a coward," fell exclaiming, "Brother, I am a dead man!" His brother carried him to a nearby house, and Captain Sloan of Hudson guarded the body at personal risk. This killing was part of broader unrest against the feudal-like manor leasehold system in the Hudson Valley, where tenants resisted evictions.

Background

Cornelius Hogeboom was a veteran of the American Revolutionary War, having served in the conflict before entering public life. He belonged to one of the oldest Knickerbocker (Dutch settler) families in New York, descending from Killian Hogeboom, who immigrated from Holland after 1712 and settled in Claverack. His lineage included Jeremiah Hogeboom (a colonial regiment colonel in 1772) and his father, Johannes Hogeboom, an early settler who acquired large tracts in Ghent before 1750 and built a stone house that became a notable inn on the Boston-Albany road. Cornelius inherited and lived on a farm in Claverack (later occupied by Henry R. Coburn in Ghent), part of the family lands divided among Johannes's sons.

Details on his childhood are sparse, with no specific anecdotes recorded; he grew up in the rural, post-colonial Hudson Valley amid a prominent Dutch-American family involved in farming and local affairs. He married Sarah Hogeboom (maiden name not specified, but possibly a relative given the shared surname), and they had no children mentioned in records. Extended family included a brother (present at his death), and he was the grandfather of Judge Henry Hogeboom (a New York Supreme Court justice) and William A. Porter (who studied law under Judge Hogeboom and regarded Cornelius as his maternal uncle). He also had a cousin named Cornelius Hogeboom mentioned in family genealogies. A stone house associated with the family in Ghent is now occupied by his great-grandson, Hon. J. T. Hogeboom.

Hogeboom's entry into law enforcement isn't explicitly motivated in records, but his early civic involvement suggests a sense of duty; on May 9, 1785, he was elected as an assistant in Hudson's first charter election alongside figures like Dirck Delamater and Peter Hogeboom Jr. This role likely paved the way for his appointment as high sheriff of Columbia County, a position he held for several years (upwards of two, per some accounts) in a time of social tension. Accomplishments include his effective service as sheriff, where he was praised for "unexampled benevolence to the distressed," "promptitude in duties," and giving "universal satisfaction" as a "real patriot and true friend." No personal controversies are documented; he navigated the volatile anti-rent period with respect from the community, though the role inherently involved enforcing unpopular evictions.

Death

Hogeboom died from a single musket ball through the heart during the ambush described above. The attack occurred near the Jordan neighborhood, close to the Claverack town line, as he and his companions (including his brother) were leaving the area on horseback. Evidence later suggested Jonathan Arnold loaded his gun with a real ball while others used blanks, but the shooter was never definitively identified. His body was interred at Squampommock (likely a local burial ground) with "uncommon respect" from the inhabitants, reflecting the community's shock at the "barbarous and inhuman" murder.

Aftermath

Immediately after, a posse was organized, leading to the arrest of 13 men (including Jonathan Arnold and others like Thomas Southward, John West, and Abel Hackett), who were indicted in December 1791—Southward as principal in the first degree and nine as accessories. Four suspects fled to Nova Scotia via New London, prompting a £250 reward for their capture, but it's unclear if they were apprehended. The trial occurred in February 1792 at the Court of Oyer and Terminer in Claverack, presided over by judges including John Lansing, Peter Van Schaack, and others. After a "long and impartial trial," all defendants were acquitted, with the jury verdict stating, "We find the prisoners at the bar not guilty, and that he did not fly for it." Local jury bias, amid Federalist vs. Anti-Federalist tensions over land rights, may have influenced the outcome.

Hogeboom's wife, Sarah, died of grief on January 16, 1792, less than three months later, as noted in the Hudson Gazette. The event shocked the region and led to "vigorous measures" by authorities that suppressed anti-rent disturbances for over half a century, preventing a larger revolt—no major incidents recurred until the mid-19th century. No specific laws were changed directly as a result, though it highlighted the need for stronger enforcement in land disputes. In modern times, Hogeboom has been honored as one of the earliest line-of-duty deaths, including a 2025 commemoration by the Columbia County Sheriff's Office. He is buried in Ghent Union Cemetery.

U.S. Marshal Robert Forsyth


Augusta, GA — January 11, 1794
Age: 40
Gender: Male
Cause of Death: shot
Killed By: Beverley Allan

On January 11, 1794, in Augusta, Georgia, U.S. Marshal Robert Forsyth, accompanied by two deputies, attempted to serve civil court papers on brothers Beverly and William Allen at the home of a widow named Mrs. Dixon. Beverly Allen, hiding on the second floor, fired a pistol through a door, striking Forsyth in the head and killing him instantly. The deputies arrested Allen, but he later escaped custody. This marked the first instance of a U.S. Marshal being killed in the line of duty, setting a somber precedent for the over 200 marshals and deputies who would follow in similar tragedies. The event occurred amid the challenges of enforcing federal law in a post-Revolutionary South, where civil disputes could quickly turn violent. It also reflects the rudimentary nature of law enforcement at the time: no warrants requiring forced entry, limited communication, and reliance on personal bravery. In a broader context, this incident symbolized the tensions between federal authority and local autonomy in the early Republic, potentially fueled by economic grievances like debt or land disputes that the Allens may have faced.

Background

Robert Forsyth's life was a story of immigration, military valor, and civic dedication, embodying the opportunities and hardships of early American settlers. Born in Scotland in 1754, he emigrated to the United States as a teenager with his family, initially settling in New England before relocating to Fredericksburg, Virginia, in 1774. This move was likely driven by economic prospects or family ties, common for Scottish immigrants fleeing post-Jacobite unrest or seeking land in the colonies. Little is documented about his early childhood, but growing up in Scotland during the Enlightenment era may have instilled values of education and public service, influencing his later roles.

His family details are sparse beyond his immediate survivors: he left behind a widow (whose name is not recorded in primary sources) and two sons. One son, John Forsyth (1780–1841), achieved prominence as Governor of Georgia (1827–1829), U.S. Minister to Spain (where he negotiated the 1819 Adams-Onís Treaty, ceding Florida to the U.S.), and Secretary of State under Presidents Andrew Jackson and Martin Van Buren. This familial legacy suggests Forsyth provided a foundation of stability and ambition, perhaps through his own accomplishments.

Forsyth's entry into public service began with patriotism during the Revolutionary War. At age 22, he enlisted in the Continental Army in 1776, motivated by the revolutionary ideals of liberty and independence—common drivers for many immigrants seeking to prove loyalty to their new homeland. In 1779, he was commissioned as a Captain in the Corps of Partisan Light Dragoons (Lee's Legion) under Major Henry "Light-Horse Harry" Lee, a daring cavalry unit known for guerrilla tactics. He later resigned to serve as aide-de-camp to General Avery, provisioning the Southern Army, and was promoted to Major of the First Virginia Legion in 1781. This military career not only honed his leadership skills but also built networks that aided his postwar life.

After the war, Forsyth returned to Fredericksburg but moved to Augusta, Georgia, in 1785, possibly attracted by land grants for veterans or economic opportunities in the expanding South. There, he thrived in civic roles: serving on the Board of Commissions, as a tax assessor, justice of the peace, and trustee of the Richmond Academy. By 1792, he owned 6,000 acres of land, indicating success in agriculture or speculation. He was also active in fraternal organizations, as a member of the Society of the Cincinnati (a veterans' group) and the Masons, where he rose to Master of Lodge Columbia and Deputy Grand Master for Georgia. These affiliations underscore his integration into elite social circles, fostering community ties and influence.

His appointment as U.S. Marshal for Georgia on September 26, 1789, by President George Washington (at age 35) was a natural progression from his military and civic background. As one of the first 16 marshals under the Judiciary Act of 1789, Forsyth was chosen for his proven reliability and leadership. Why he joined: Beyond duty, it offered prestige and a federal salary in a time when local offices were unstable. Accomplishments included enforcing early federal laws, such as census duties and court processes, contributing to the stabilization of the new government. Edge cases: His immigrant status might have faced xenophobia, yet his war service mitigated this. Related considerations: Forsyth's life exemplifies the "self-made man" archetype, but it also highlights inequalities—his success relied on networks unavailable to all, like enslaved labor on his lands (though not explicitly documented, common in Georgia).

Death

Forsyth died from a gunshot wound to the head. The fatal shot was fired at close range during an attempt to serve civil court papers, resulting in instantaneous death. This cause reflects the violent nature of law enforcement in the late 18th century, where officers often operated without modern protections like body armor or backup protocols. Medically, a head wound from a pistol ball in that era would have been invariably fatal due to limited medical knowledge—no antibiotics, surgical techniques, or trauma care existed to mitigate infection or brain damage. From a historical angle, this incident underscores the risks of enforcing federal authority in a young nation still grappling with local resistances, such as tax disputes or civil suits that could escalate into deadly confrontations. It also parallels other early American law enforcement deaths, where firearms were the primary weapon, emphasizing the personal vulnerability of marshals who lacked institutional safeguards.

Aftermath

The aftermath of Forsyth's death rippled through his family, the justice system, and historical memory, though no direct legislative changes are recorded. Left behind were his widow and two young sons, including John (then about 14), who would carry forward the family name through political achievements. The widow's fate is undocumented, but as a single mother in 1790s Georgia, she likely faced financial hardships, relying on community support or Forsyth's estate (including his 6,000 acres).

Beverly Allen was tried and found guilty, but he escaped prison after just six weeks, reportedly with friends' help. A reward was offered via the Kentucky Gazette in May 1794, but he evaded capture by fleeing to the wilderness of Logan County, Kentucky (then a haven for fugitives known as "Rogue's Harbor"). There, Allen reinvented himself: practicing medicine, accumulating wealth through land investments along Big Whippoorwill Creek, heading a school (where future evangelist Peter Cartwright studied), and returning to religious preaching. Described during his flight as pale and emaciated but retaining a "fine appearance," he even stopped at a Presbyterian meeting to declare his conversion, eliciting emotional responses from attendees. He wrote his will in October 1816 and died in February 1817, buried in Allen Cemetery near Russellville. This escape highlights the limitations of early American justice—porous prisons, vast frontiers for hiding, and social complicity—allowing Allen a second life spanning 23 years.

No specific laws were changed directly as a result, per available records; the incident did not prompt immediate federal reforms like enhanced marshal protections or escape prevention measures. However, it contributed to the evolving narrative of federal law enforcement's dangers, potentially influencing the gradual professionalization of the U.S. Marshals Service over the 19th century. Broader implications include heightened awareness of officer safety, echoed in modern memorials and training. Edge cases: Allen's successful evasion might have discouraged pursuits of fugitives, while Forsyth's legacy endures through his son's treaties and the 2008 historical marker in Augusta. In 2012, retired Deputy U.S. Marshal Rom Latham researched Allen's fate, confirming details via archives and descendants, closing a historical loop.

Deputy Sheriff Robert Berwick


Manhattan, New York — June 29, 1797
Age: Unknown
Gender: Male
Cause of Death: shot
Killed By: John Young

On Broadway near St. Paul's Church, Deputy Sheriff Robert Berwick was shot and killed while attempting to serve a writ (civil court papers, essentially a warrant for re-arrest related to debt). John Young had only recently been released from debtor's prison when Berwick approached him for another outstanding debt. During the confrontation, Young shot Berwick. The incident occurred in the course of routine civil process serving, a primary duty of sheriffs and deputies in the post-Revolutionary era when debtor's imprisonment was still common practice.

Background

Very little personal biographical information survives about Robert Berwick, as was typical for many low-profile local law enforcement officers of the late 18th century. He served as a deputy sheriff in New York County's Sheriff's Office, an appointed position responsible for serving civil writs, making arrests for debt, and supporting court functions. No records detail his birth date, place of origin, prior occupation, or exact reasons for joining the force. In that era, deputy sheriffs were often local men appointed by the elected sheriff to handle day-to-day civil enforcement; it was not a full-time "police" role in the modern sense but a civic duty tied to the legal system. There is no indication of military service, family political connections, or other background that prompted his appointment.

Death

Deputy Berwick died instantly (or shortly after) from the gunshot wound on June 29, 1797, while performing his official duties. He was survived by his wife (name not recorded in public memorials).

Aftermath

John Young was quickly arrested, tried, convicted of murder, and hanged on August 17, 1797, in New York City. A contemporary pamphlet titled Narrative of the life, last dying speech & confession of John Young was published on the day of his execution, detailing his account of the events. Young was described as having just been released from debtor's prison prior to the fatal re-arrest attempt. Berwick's widow was directly affected, left without her husband. No children or other family members are mentioned in surviving records.

There were no documented changes to laws or policing procedures as a direct result of this killing. It remained a tragic but isolated case of resistance during a civil debt enforcement, which was a common (and often contentious) part of early American sheriff work. The incident was not widely sensationalized beyond the execution pamphlet and local records. Berwick's death was officially recognized by the Officer Down Memorial Page and the National Law Enforcement Officers Memorial Fund (added to the memorial wall in recent years, around 2020). No broader reforms to debtor arrest practices or law enforcement protections stemmed from it; debtor's prison itself persisted in various forms into the 19th century.
