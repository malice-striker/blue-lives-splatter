Constable Darius Quimby

Stephentown, NY
January 3, 1791
Age: 20
Gender: Male
Cause of Death: stabbing
Killed By: Whiting Sweeting

Darius Quimby holds the distinction of being recognized as the first known law enforcement officer killed in the line of duty in the United States, an event that occurred shortly after the nation's founding and has been memorialized in various historical accounts and police honors. His story is often cited in discussions of early American policing and the dangers faced by constables in frontier communities.

Background

Quimby was born around 1760 (though some accounts suggest circa 1771) in or near Stephentown, which was then part of Albany County, New York (now Rensselaer County). He was the son of Ephraim Quimby (circa 1730–1824), a settler in the region who married Elisabeth Pepper around 1760. The Quimby family traced its roots to English immigrants William and Robert Quinby, who arrived in New England in the 1630s, and they were part of the broader colonial settlement in New York and New England during the post-Revolutionary era. The 1790 U.S. Census records Ephraim as head of household in Stephentown, Albany County, listing Darius as a free white male over 16, confirming his adulthood and eligibility for community roles like constable. Little is documented about his siblings or extended family beyond this, but the Quimbys were typical of frontier families involved in farming and local trades.

Specific motivations for Quimby becoming a constable are not detailed in historical records, but constables in early America were often community members elected or appointed to maintain order, serve warrants, and handle minor disputes—roles that were typically unpaid or minimally compensated. As a young man in his 20s or early 30s living in a rural, post-Revolutionary settlement, he likely took on the position as a civic duty, common for eligible males in small towns where formal policing was rudimentary. Prior to his death, Quimby was engaged in manual labor, such as making wood shingles with two other men, indicating he was not a full-time officer but rather a part-time or volunteer peacekeeper.

His primary "accomplishment" is posthumous: being etched into history as the inaugural line-of-duty death for U.S. law enforcement, a milestone that has led to his name appearing on memorials, such as those honoring fallen officers. There are no records of notable prior achievements, arrests, or cases he handled, reflecting the modest scope of constabulary work in 1790s rural New York. His brief service underscores the informal nature of early American law enforcement, where ordinary citizens like Quimby stepped up for community needs.

Death

On January 3, 1791, Quimby was part of a posse led by Constable John McCarty attempting to serve a trespassing warrant on Whiting Sweeting, a 26-year-old local resident, in the area near Stephentown. Quimby encountered the group while working on shingles and voluntarily joined the pursuit. Sweeting, upon spotting the posse, fled into the snowy woods at night and climbed onto a rock, arming himself with a club and knife while threatening to harm anyone who approached. Undeterred, Quimby grabbed Sweeting and pulled him down, leading to a violent struggle in the snow. During the scuffle, Sweeting stabbed Quimby twice in the right side with a knife, inflicting fatal wounds. Quimby succumbed to his injuries shortly after, marking the incident as a stabbing death in the line of duty.

Aftermath

Historical records do not mention Quimby having a wife or children of his own at the time of his death, suggesting he may have been unmarried or without documented dependents. He left behind his father, Ephraim, and likely other family members in the Stephentown area, as indicated by the 1790 census. The family's colonial roots imply siblings or relatives in New England, but specifics are sparse, emphasizing the limited documentation of ordinary lives in the era.

Quimby's death prompted swift legal action: Sweeting was arrested, tried for murder in the New York Supreme Court during the July 1791 term, convicted, and hanged publicly in Albany on August 26, 1791. The case highlighted the perils of early law enforcement and set a precedent for recognizing officer deaths. In modern times, Quimby's legacy is preserved through memorials, such as the National Law Enforcement Officers Memorial, and local commemorations like exhibits at the Stephentown Heritage Center. His story has been referenced in police training, historical writings, and annual remembrances during National Police Week, serving as a symbol of sacrifice in the nascent United States.
